<Section icon={Swords} title="Sokka Moves and Core Game Plan">

The simplest way to play Sokka is to build every round around three questions: where is the opponent standing, how do I make them uncomfortable, and what do I spend once I get a hit? That is the heart of his route to damage. He is not a character for random aggression. He is a character for forcing bad movement and then cashing out.

<Callout type="warning" title="Avoid this mistake">
Do not chase damage before you control space. Sokka usually does better when he wins neutral, then spends resources after a confirmed opening.
</Callout>

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Learn your safe check">
    Start with the button or sequence that reaches the farthest without overcommitting. Your goal is to stop the opponent from walking freely.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Add movement layers">
    Use Flow movement and angle changes to avoid predictable pressure. Sokka gets better the less obvious your movement becomes.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Confirm before spending">
    Hold meter or Energy until you actually have advantage. Clean confirms are worth more than flashy guesses.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Reset to control">
    After damage, return to spacing and make the next interaction uncomfortable again. Sokka wins by repeating the cycle.
  </Step>
</Steps>

| Situation | Better choice | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Opponent is walking forward | **Check with range** | Forces respect and slows their approach |
| Opponent is rushing | **Reposition first** | Keeps you from getting trapped |
| You land a hit | **Confirm before spending** | Preserves resources for real openings |
| You are ahead | **Play neutral, not panic** | Keeps your lead from collapsing |
| You are cornered | **Escape angle, then reset** | Sokka is better when he can move |

Sokka’s weapon focus also makes him easy to understand in training. If a string feels unreliable, trim it down until your neutral becomes repeatable. In fighting games, repeatable beats impressive more often than people admit.

</Section>
